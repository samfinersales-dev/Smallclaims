Small Claims Court in Florida: Limits, Forms, and Updated Rules: Complete Guide

April 20, 2026SmallClaimsHelper4 min read

Filing a small claims case in Florida doesn't have to be overwhelming, even if you've never set foot in a courtroom before. This guide walks you through everything you need to know — from the current dollar limits and required forms to the latest rule updates and what happens after you win your judgment.

Florida Small Claims Court Limit and What You Can Sue For

Florida's small claims court handles civil disputes involving $8,000 or less, not including court costs and attorney's fees. This makes it an accessible option for everyday disputes without hiring a lawyer.

Common cases filed in Florida small claims court include:

  • Unpaid loans between individuals or businesses
  • Security deposit disputes between tenants and landlords
  • Property damage claims
  • Unpaid services or contracts
  • Bad checks or returned payments

You cannot use small claims court to sue for defamation, seek an injunction, or file cases involving title to real property. If your claim exceeds $8,000, you would need to file in county civil court instead. Always confirm the current limit with your local courthouse before filing.

What Changed in Florida's Small Claims Rules in 2026

Florida's small claims procedures have seen notable updates heading into 2026 that filers should understand before submitting paperwork.

Key changes and developments to be aware of include:

  • The Florida Supreme Court has continued refining e-filing requirements, making online submission the standard in most counties
  • Pretrial conference procedures have been updated to encourage earlier settlement between parties
  • Clerks are providing more standardized guidance to self-represented litigants at the point of filing
  • Service of process rules have been clarified for cases involving businesses and registered agents

These updates are designed to make the process faster and more accessible for people representing themselves. Always check the Florida Rules of Civil Procedure for Small Claims (Rules 7.010–7.350) and your specific county court's website for the most current local requirements before you file.

How to File a Small Claims Case in Florida: Step-by-Step

Filing a small claims case in Florida follows a clear process that most people can handle without an attorney.

  • Step 1: Gather your evidence, including contracts, receipts, photos, texts, and any written agreements related to your dispute
  • Step 2: Complete the Statement of Claim form (Form 7.322), available from your county clerk's office or online
  • Step 3: File your completed forms at the clerk's office in the county where the defendant lives or where the dispute occurred
  • Step 4: Pay the filing fee, which typically ranges from $30 to $100 depending on your claim amount
  • Step 5: Receive your case number and hearing date from the clerk

Filing in the correct county is important. Filing in the wrong jurisdiction can result in your case being dismissed or transferred, causing unnecessary delays.

Serving the Defendant and Attending Your Pretrial Conference

After filing, the defendant must be officially notified about the lawsuit through a process called service of process.

In Florida small claims cases, service is typically handled by the county sheriff or a certified process server. You will need to provide the defendant's correct address and pay a service fee, usually between $30 and $50.

What to expect at your pretrial conference:

  • Both parties appear before a judge or magistrate
  • The judge will encourage settlement before trial
  • You should bring all your evidence and documentation
  • If the defendant does not appear, you may be awarded a default judgment

Many small claims cases settle at the pretrial conference stage, so come prepared to negotiate. If no agreement is reached, the judge will schedule a formal trial date where both sides present their full case.

Collecting Your Judgment After You Win

Winning a judgment is a significant step, but it does not automatically mean you will receive your money. You are responsible for collecting it.

Florida gives judgment winners several legal tools to recover what they are owed:

  • Writ of Execution: Allows the sheriff to seize and sell the debtor's non-exempt property
  • Wage Garnishment: Lets you collect directly from the debtor's paycheck, subject to Florida exemption rules
  • Bank Levy: Enables you to freeze and collect funds from the debtor's bank account
  • Judgment Lien: Attaches to the debtor's real property in the county where it is recorded

Be aware that Florida has strong debtor protections, including the homestead exemption. If the defendant claims they have no collectible assets, you may need to conduct a debtor's examination through the court to identify what they own.

Frequently asked questions

What is the maximum amount I can sue for in Florida small claims court?

Florida small claims court handles cases involving $8,000 or less, not counting court costs and attorney's fees. If your claim is higher than that amount, you will need to file in the county civil division instead.

Do I need a lawyer to file a small claims case in Florida?

No, you do not need a lawyer. Florida's small claims court is specifically designed to be accessible to self-represented individuals. Attorneys are permitted but not required, and most people successfully handle their own cases with the right preparation and properly completed forms.

What happens if the defendant doesn't show up to the pretrial conference?

If the defendant fails to appear at the scheduled pretrial conference without a valid reason, the judge can enter a default judgment in your favor. This means you win the case automatically, though you will still need to take steps to collect the money owed to you.
